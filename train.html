Train
Sat 25 March 2017 Writing short

From a certain very entertaining game, I tried writing a little more on the story.

I took a sip. Since my promotion at work two years ago, sleep became a precious commodity. From the recommendations of my cousins I started drinking elderflower tea, which helped with sinuses problems and the flu that began to plague my health. I took another sip.

Brigid was looking at me, golden irises radiant as ever. Today though, they seemed disturbed. The sky outside being completely overcast did nothing to help the mood. In fact we were, and are, lucky that it has not began raining yet.

“Do you believe in destiny?” she asks.

Destiny. Some more devout at the church may say that our lives are predetermined, that regardless of our choice, we would follow a path that is already set out for us. Others call that a pile of rubbish. Myself, I’m not so sure. The first time I met Brigid, she seemed so radiant, with a presence so strong that I felt myself drawn to her. That she was a capable woman did nothing to thwart that fact, which led me to that cafe by the station.

I nodded. “Yes, to some extent I feel that some things are predetermined.” What had brought that up though?

She sighed deeply, shaking her head. “You need to take control, or you’ll find yourself lost without realizing it. Life is like a boat John. Sometimes the currents are strong, but without paddling your oars you’ll find yourself casted to a far unknown sea.”

Looking back at me, she continued with more intensity. “Destiny is an illusion. You’re the one in control. You’re the one who chose to bring me out to this quaint cafe, you’re the one who chose to stroll though the park before getting here. And just like me. There are many drinks I could have ordered John. But I ordered this.”

Hard to read as ever. Brigid may be capable, but her mind worked in unexpected ways. An unusual suspect, my boss would say. No one else had thought to ask the janitor how he felt the office was better arranged. And now our office was to be rearranged over the weekend.

“John.” she started again, voice wavering.

“Yes?” I replied, wondering what she was thinking this time.

“I need you to do three things for me.”
